Visual Assist

Mobile App Design

Overview

Problem

People with vision impairments either cannot read, or struggle to read, aisle signs or labels which helps them find the products they need.

Solution

Design an application that allows people with vision impairments to use their mobile device to help navigate or find products in a Trader Joe’s grocery store.

Role

User Persona, Ideas Clustering, User Flow Diagram, Wireframing, and Prototyping

Time

3 weeks

Tools

Miro, Figma, Trello, InVision, Smartdraw, Photoshop, Pen & Paper

Team

Edward McCarthy, Nurdan Ayres, Kristoffer Sayoc

Process

1. Research

The objective for this project was to design a mobile app that solves a real need in a person's day-to-day life. The group decided to tackle the unmet needs of people with vision impairment as they perform their grocery shopping.

Among the key insights observed from the research are:

  • Visually impaired shoppers prefers to select their own products because they don't think others will pick the best options available.
  • 61.54% among the survey respondents indicated they prefer to purchase their groceries on a supermarket.
  • 38.46% among the survey participants indicated that the top obstacle they face when shopping is looking for specific items.

2. Definition and Ideation

To personify the data gathered from the research phase, I led the creation of a user persona named Jose Andres.

Like us, Jose wants to make sure the grocery items he orders are of the best quality. Jose has inspired us to think of solutions to help him do his grocery shopping with ease.

Shown above are the ideas (or solutions) we have generated on the ideation phase. One thing we learned is that there could be so many ways of helping Jose do his grocery shopping.

We believe that Trader Joe’s is not currently offering a video call assistance feature, and that it would greatly help visually impaired customers do their own grocery shopping.

  • a scheduler
  • a waitlist for those that doesn't have an appointment

4. Testing and Iteration

To identify user trends and observe user behavior, the group performed guerilla user testing.

From the guerrilla user tests, we learned that we cannot assume that the user can find their way through an application without providing an explanation of where they are, or what they need to do.

Even though a designer may think a prototype is clear and concise, a new user may stumble and be confused when explanations are missing.

Final Thoughts

Topic of the project

As sighted individuals, we often forget about the needs of our visually impaired counterparts. From grocery shopping to app design, we should provide assistance when we are able to.

I learned that even the smallest ambiguity can lead to confusion. It is best to present only the information that is needed (on a page), on mediums that they can clearly see or hear.

Team + Work

One of the difficulty we had in doing this case study is that in our individual prototypes (not shown), we had different methods of performing a single task (e.g. specifying an item through a grocery list vs. adding items to a cart).

This could have been avoided if we had agreed upon on a single user flow before we made the prototypes.

Sticking with the scheduler (Trello)

I believe our work in the group would have been more uncomplicated if we had just set and followed deadlines on Trello.

We could have also better utilized the “blocks” list on Trello, to indicate where parts of the case study a team member may be stuck on and thus, needs help.

Changes to the focus of the project and/or limitations

We had to change course midway, from focusing on the video call assistance, to the scheduling part, because of time constraints and limited testing opportunity.
